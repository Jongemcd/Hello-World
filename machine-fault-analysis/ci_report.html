Executive summary

Of the 2,426.7 hours of master downtime logged over the ~7-month period, only 979.0 hours (40.3%) reflects a genuine equipment or process failure. An almost equal share — 992.5 hours (40.9%) — is guard/door interlocks opening, logged as "downtime" but driven by very short, very frequent operator access events (53,089 of them, ~67 seconds each on average), not breakdowns. The rest is pre-warnings (10.8%), operator/procedural messages (4.4%), and changeover/recipe mismatches (3.5%).

Once that noise is removed, the picture changes completely: @Fault_0081 / @Fault_0085 ("waiting for Schubert") is not just one line item among many — at 221.1 hours it is larger than the next nine real technical fault families combined (160.3 hrs). The master-child hand-off is, by a wide margin, the single biggest true reliability problem on this line, which is exactly why the linked root-cause breakdown in this report (and the dashboard built earlier) matters most.

Statistical significance: is this linkage real, or coincidental timing?

A raw count inside the stoppage windows can't tell you whether a child alarm is actually tied to the master's stoppage or just happens a lot on Schubert regardless. Two things push back on treating "biggest count in the window" as "root cause": some alarms (like "No product from upstream machine") are so frequent across the entire child log that they'll rack up a large count inside any sufficiently long set of windows by pure chance. And because we have the complete population of events — not a sample — the usual sampling-based p-value ("how likely is this result if we'd drawn a different random sample") doesn't apply. The question that does apply: if the timing of these master stoppages had nothing to do with this child alarm, how often would we see a count this large just from where the alarm's occurrences happen to fall on the clock? That's a Poisson enrichment test, and it's the right tool here.

Method: for each child alarm type, its background rate λ = (total occurrences across the full 174.8-day child log) ÷ (log duration in seconds). Expected count in-window = λ × 418.4 hours (the total ±30s-padded exposure time of all 13,596 @Fault_0081/@Fault_0085 windows). Observed vs. expected is tested with a one-sided Poisson test (P(Poisson(expected) ≥ observed)); with 96 alarm types tested at once, a Bonferroni-corrected threshold of α = 5.2×10⁻⁴ is used instead of the usual 0.05, so a "significant" result here is significant by a strict standard. Two of the highest-count categories were spot-checked with an independent 1,000-trial Monte Carlo permutation test (randomly re-timing the same windows across the child log's timeline) — it agreed with the Poisson result in both cases (p = 0.001).

"Expected by chance," in plain English: it's how many times we'd see a given alarm during the master's stoppages even if that alarm had nothing to do with the stoppages at all — just because that alarm happens a lot in general. Some alarms fire constantly, all day, no matter what. So even a random slice of time will catch a bunch of them — not because that slice is special, but because the alarm is everywhere. "Expected by chance" is that catch-anyway number.

For example: "Overflow container empty" fired 91,100 times over the whole ~6 months. The stoppage windows only cover about 10% of that time, so pure luck alone predicts roughly 9,088 of those 91,100 should land inside the windows — 10% of a thing that happens everywhere lands in any 10% slice, stoppage or not. We actually counted 7,082 inside the windows — at or below that luck-alone number. So this alarm isn't specially tied to the stoppages; it's just common everywhere.

Compare that to "Pick-up error, literature Track 1": rare overall, but it showed up 22× more often inside the stoppage windows than luck alone would predict. That gap — way more than chance explains — is the signal of a real connection, not a coincidence.

The headline correction: biggest count ≠ statistically real cause

"Overflow container for grouping belt empty," "Local Sercos bus does not run as ring," and "Refill magazine" — the three largest raw counts in the linked Pareto above — all come back not statistically significant. Each occurs so often across the whole child log that its in-window count is at or below what pure chance predicts (rate ratio 0.72–0.78, p = 1.0). They are common Schubert conditions, period — not conditions specifically tied to the master waiting on Schubert. "No product from upstream machine" and both VAPRO production-mode messages land the same way, consistent with the read in the section above.

Is "guard/interlock" really a different kind of stop than "technical failure"?

The noise classification treats guard/door interlocks and confirmed technical failures as different populations. A Mann-Whitney U test on their stoppage-duration distributions (53,089 interlock stops vs. 181,835 technical-failure stops) confirms they are statistically distinguishable (U = 6.85×10⁹, p < 10⁻³⁰⁰, rank-biserial effect size r = 0.42 — a medium-to-large effect). The direction is worth reading carefully, though: interlock stops have the longer median (22.0s vs. 2.3s) and mean (67.3s vs. 19.4s). Technical Failure's short median comes from a large tail of very brief, flickering entries — the same instrumentation-chatter pattern flagged in the HU3 report — pulling its typical duration down even though it still contains the longest genuine outages. With sample sizes this large, statistical significance alone is close to guaranteed; the medians and means, not just the p-value, are what's actually informative here.

On p-values and population data, briefly: every number in this report comes from the complete logged history, not a sample drawn from a larger population — so these tests don't answer "would this hold up in a different sample," they answer "could a pattern this strong show up if the two things being compared were actually unrelated, given how they're timed or distributed." That's the correct question for event-log correlation and duration-comparison work like this, and it's what every test in this section is built around.
